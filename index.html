Jawaharlal Nehru: 15 Key Milestones

Born on November 14 to Motilal Nehru and Swarup Rani. Raised in a wealthy, privileged household, his early education was managed by private tutors at home.

Traveled to England to study at Harrow School. He later graduated with an honors degree in natural sciences from Trinity College, Cambridge, and completed his legal training at the Inner Temple.

Returned to India to practice law at the Allahabad High Court. He attended his first Indian National Congress (INC) session in Bankipore, finding it initially too elite and detached.

Met Mahatma Gandhi for the first time at the Lucknow Congress session. Gandhi's radical approach to passive resistance deeply impressed him, shifting his political focus entirely toward the nationalist struggle.

Organized and led the peasant movement in the Awadh region of Uttar Pradesh. This firsthand exposure to rural poverty transformed his worldview, cementing his lifelong commitment to socialist economic principles.

Arrested for the first time for participating in the Non-Cooperation Movement. This marked the beginning of a long series of jail terms, spending a total of over nine years in prison across his lifetime.

Presided over the historic Lahore session of the Congress, where he drafted and passed the resolution demanding "Purna Swaraj" (Complete Independence) instead of dominion status.

Elected Congress President again at the Lucknow session. He urged the party to adopt a socialist framework to tackle land issues and poverty, bridging the gap between mainstream politicians and trade unions.

Arrested along with other top Congress leaders for backing Gandhi's Quit India resolution. He was detained at Ahmednagar Fort until 1945, his longest single stretch of imprisonment.

Took charge as the Vice President of the Governor-General's Executive Council, effectively serving as the leader of India's Interim Government to oversee the complex transition to independence.

Sworn in as the first Prime Minister of independent India on August 15. He delivered his iconic "Tryst with Destiny" speech to Parliament as the nation emerged from colonial rule amid the crisis of Partition.

Launched the First Five-Year Plan, prioritizing agricultural development and major irrigation projects like the Bhakra-Nangal Dam, which he famously termed the "temples of modern India."

Signed the Five Principles of Peaceful Coexistence (Panchsheel) with China, attempting to establish a framework for peaceful diplomatic relationships and border stability in Asia.

Played a central role at the Afro-Asian Conference in Bandung, Indonesia. This paved the way for the formal creation of the Non-Aligned Movement, charting a neutral course away from US-Soviet blocs.

Passed away on May 27 in New Delhi after a stroke. He left behind an institutional legacy that shaped modern India, including democratic norms, economic infrastructure, and a push for scientific research.
